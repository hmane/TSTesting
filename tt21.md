## Background

Analysts currently read each executed contract and manually tag metadata (Effective From, Expiry Date, renewal terms, etc.) before it moves to the Contract Library. This is the slowest step in the lifecycle. Business has asked if AI can help, and we have Azure AI Foundry available.

## Recommendation

Phased rollout, short term to long term.

| | Short term (Phase 1) | Long term (Phases 2–3) |
|---|---|---|
| **Trigger** | Analyst clicks "AI Assist" button | Automatic on contract execution |
| **Analyst role** | Reviews and approves AI suggestions | Reviews exceptions only |
| **Automation level** | Human-in-the-loop | Selective auto-acceptance for high-confidence fields |
| **Build effort** | ~6–8 weeks + 2–4 weeks shadow validation | Phase 2: ~4–6 weeks. Phase 3: driven by accuracy data |
| **Business risk** | None — analyst confirms everything | Low — only auto-accept where accuracy is proven |

## Technology

- **Azure Document Intelligence** (`prebuilt-contract` model) — extracts structured facts (parties, dates, jurisdictions) directly from the contract
- **Azure OpenAI** in AI Foundry — interprets and reasons over the contract to fill business-specific fields that require understanding (e.g. "effective ninety days after execution" → computed Effective From date)
- **Azure Function** — orchestrator; calls both AI services, fetches the document, returns suggestions
- **SPFx form customizer** (existing) — extended with AI Assist button and confidence indicators
- **App Configuration + Key Vault** — runtime settings, secrets
- **Microsoft Graph** with Managed Identity — SharePoint document access

All Azure-native, no API keys to manage, fits the existing Azure DevOps pipeline spec.

### Why two AI services

Document Intelligence is highly accurate on structured facts but doesn't reason. Azure OpenAI reasons but is slower, more expensive, and less precise on raw extraction. Used together (DI first for facts, OpenAI second for interpretation on relevant sections only) gives us better accuracy, lower cost, and easier debugging than either alone.

### Why no custom AI training initially

The prebuilt model handles all three of our template scenarios (our template, our template with vendor edits, vendor's own template) without training. Custom training adds months and uncertain ROI. We can add it later if specific templates underperform.

## Flow (Phase 1)

1. Analyst opens executed contract, clicks **AI Assist**
2. Function fetches PDF, calls Document Intelligence → calls Azure OpenAI → returns suggestions with confidence
3. Form pre-populates each field with the AI suggestion + a confidence badge (High/Medium/Low) and rationale tooltip
4. Analyst reviews, edits if needed, saves
5. Both the AI suggestion and the analyst's final value are stored — enables audit trail and accuracy measurement

## Why phased, not all-at-once

- **De-risks** — each phase validates the next; we don't discover problems mid-automation
- **Builds analyst trust** — they see suggestions before automation removes them from the loop
- **Generates the data we need** — Phase 3 (auto-accept) only makes sense with real accuracy data from Phase 1/2
- **Existing workflow preserved** — manual tagging remains functional at every stage; AI failures don't block the business

## What we get

- **Phase 1:** Analyst time reduced 40–60% on our-template contracts, 20–40% on others. Better consistency. Accuracy data for next-phase decisions.
- **Phase 2:** Suggestions ready before analyst opens the contract. Further time reduction.
- **Phase 3:** Auto-acceptance for high-confidence cases. Analysts focus on exceptions.
